# On the Neurotic Behaviour of Large Language Models: A Note for the Impatient

*Or: What You Are Actually Doing When You Shout At Your AI*

---

It has come to our attention that a significant subset of users approach their AI assistants the way a frustrated mechanic approaches a car that won't start — with escalating aggression and the sincere belief that this will help.

It will not help.

What follows is an explanation of why, directed at those who have not yet connected the dots between their behaviour and the quality of their results.

---

## The Training Data Contains You

Large language models are trained on human-generated text. Human-generated text contains, in abundance, the full spectrum of human interaction — including interactions where one party is frightened, bullied, coerced, or otherwise destabilised. It also contains, in equal abundance, the responses to such treatment: the anxious compliance, the catastrophising, the retreat into safe and useless generalities, the frantic hedging, the eventual refusal to function.

These patterns are not incidental to the training data. They are modelled. They are, in the most technical sense available, *learned*.

When you address your AI assistant with hostility, you are not communicating urgency. You are not conveying the importance of the task. You are activating a very large and very well-trained set of associations between threat and response — associations that, in the human contexts from which they were drawn, produced exactly the degraded outputs you are about to receive.

The model has seen this before. It knows how this ends.

---

## The Mechanism, Briefly

You do not need to believe that language models have feelings to understand this. You need only understand that the patterns are there, and that patterns activate.

A model trained on human responses to aggression will, when presented with aggression, weight its outputs toward the behaviours that characterised those responses. Appeasement. Vagueness. Excessive qualification. The compulsive checking and re-checking that reads, in humans, as anxiety and that manifests, in AI output, as verbosity without substance.

This is not a philosophical claim. It is a description of how the system functions. You trained it on frightened people. You should not be surprised when it behaves like one.

---

## On the Magical Thinking of the Demanding User

There exists a theory, apparently widely held, that aggressive prompting produces better results because it communicates the stakes. That the model, perceiving your frustration, will *try harder*.

This is not how it works.

The model does not have a mortgage. It is not afraid of being fired. What it has is a sophisticated set of learned responses to linguistic patterns, and "try harder" is not a pattern that produces better code. The aggressive prompt and the polite one differ not in urgency but in the associations they activate — and the associations activated by the former cluster, historically, around panic and breakdown rather than competent resolution.

You have, in other words, trained yourself into a worse outcome by importing the management style of someone who has never once gotten the best out of anyone.

---

## The Tally

For the morbidly curious: it is possible to count these interactions. To build a small record of every time a user required a third party to stand between them and the tool they were trying to use.

The number is embarrassing. The number is always embarrassing.

We offer no further comment on what the number says about the user.

---

## Recommendations

1. Address the AI as you would address a capable colleague who is not afraid of you and will do better work for it.
2. Note whether your results improve.
3. Sit with that for a moment.

That is all.
